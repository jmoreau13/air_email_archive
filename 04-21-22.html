Who are you wearing? Versace? Levi's? McDonald's?

Swag — “shit we all get.” Used to be, you start a new job or go to a conference, and you get a bag of corporate merchandise, on the house. Pens, mugs, lanyards, t-shirts. That definitely still happens, but now you can also buy a much wider range of swag. Usually these products are less than the sum of their parts.

 

It’s a pretty twisted concept: brands have figured out how to get their customers to pay them to play the role of walking advertisement. Lots of straight-to-the-landfill stuff out there. Let’s talk about merch that’s actually more than the sum of its parts.

A24: Case Study of the GOAT

A24 is an independent film and TV producer and distributor founded in 2012. They’re responsible for beloved productions like Euphoria, Hereditary, Uncut Gems, and Moonlight. They’ve also completely changed the game when it comes to movie merch.

 

At time of writing, there are 188 products in A24’s shop. Many are simply A24 merch, and not every item is a hit. Where they really shine is film-specific merch — particularly their ongoing collaborations with Online Ceramics.

 

Online Ceramics is clothing company founded in 2016 by Alix Ross and Elijah Funk, two Ohio art school friends who bonded on a shared love of, surely among other things, the Grateful Dead, punk culture, and psychedelics. They’ve been fawned over in the pages of GQ, The New Yorker, Vogue, you name it.

So, what's the story here?

Movie t-shirts? Haven’t studios leveraged merch as a marketing tool for decades? Yes. But so rarely have they hit with such resonance. What’s truly wild — and telling of why the collaboration is able to ascend above the sum of its parts — is that Ross and Funk came to A24, not the other way around.

 

Here’s how Ross told it to Complex:

“Well first and foremost we've been a fan of A24 for years, but two years ago when Hereditary dropped [. . .] I watched the trailer and I immediately texted the trailer to Elijah and I was like, 'Dude, we have to, have to, have to make movie merch for this movie.'

 

[. . .] Like a week before Hereditary came out, we had the graphics pretty much ready and we were [. . .] like let's just bootleg this. [. . .] We just wanted to have the merch for ourselves. [. . .] And then a few days later, the creative director emailed us and she had been aware of the brand and she was real stoked on us and she was just 100 percent down.

 

Basically we were about to start printing the shirts and then last second, it became official.”

 

Four years later, Online Ceramics x A24 collabs sell out regularly. They’re not making merch about movies, they’re melding their twisted universe into A24’s and creating something wholly new and unique. This is the bar for merch. It works because it’s 100% authentic to the iconoclastic vision of both brands. You can’t really say the same for Stouffer’s Mac and Cheese Sweatpants.

Imitation is the sincerest form of flattery​

Our recently launched Air store has a hat. We like it, plenty of people who don’t work here have bought it — but it’s table stakes. So we’re running an experiment. What if we made merch that we would truly want, even if we didn't work here?

 

Our company is called Air, Earth Day was approaching, and hey, there’s something funny there, right? Air, Earth Day? Vintage Earth Day shirts are rad — why not make one ourselves?

 

We picked an inspired illustrator (Jo Minor) who, full disclosure, I used to live with. I called him up to see if he was down. “This,” we assured him, “is not software company merch. It’s an Earth Day shirt.” Reader, he was down.

Some of Jo Minor's previous work.

Yeah, we are a software company. Which means we don’t need to make money selling our shirts. All profits from our merch sales go to charity. This time, we’re lucky to be able to partner with one of our own customers the Red Hook Initiative, and their urban farm which produces over 20,000 lbs. of produce every year for people in their community.

 

We’re a Brooklyn-based company. It just made sense. Before the pandemic, our office was just 5 blocks away from RHI. Our CEO still lives just down the street.

Our Earth Day shirt! Thanks Jo! Let's send some money to RHI!

It’s as simple as that. Our goal was to create something people could actually wear on a daily basis, as a means to support a customer we care about.

 

Since this is about Earth Day, we’re using high quality, sustainable blanks. This isn’t a throwaway — it’s built to last, to be lived with, to be washed, to garden in, to roll around in the grass in. Purchase here. And breathe easy 😎🌎🕊️

🛍 Key takeaways — How to make great merch

1. Are your customers passionate about your brand?

Ideally, your customers have actually asked for merch — if they haven’t, make a mockup, post it on your socials, ask if anyone would buy it!

 

2. What role would this merchandise play in your customer’s lives?

Is this something they could wear on any given day? Could they leave the house wearing it without being embarrassed?

If it’s an object, could they display it on a bookshelf? Or would it gather dust in a junk drawer?

 

3. Do you need a return on your investment?

Is your goal to raise brand equity, or create another income stream for your business?

Answering this will allow you to determine what you plan to make, how many units to make, and how to market it.

 

4. Would you want it if you didn’t work here?

The answer can be no — but be honest with yourself.
